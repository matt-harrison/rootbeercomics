real talk note
i was writing this as a comment but it got too long.

you managed to be very honest (even if you delete this and it ends up just as honesty with yourself) and still entertaining in this note.

most of the things you mention here are things we all struggle with to varying degrees - mostly stemming from physical or intellectual vanity. i think it's part of modern american culture to worry about body image - even if you are not consciously affected by axe body spray ads, they work their way into your perceptions. every single one of us has that problem.

but it's the other type of vanity that really affects people like you and me, i think.

one part of it is the way we sculpt ourselves into the people we want to be. as a kid, i desperately wanted to be deep, adult. i showed people my terrible poetry for the same reason i hide it now (and why i can't mention it without a disclaimer about its quality): it's an effort to control how people perceive me. there's comes a moment (or perhaps a series of moments), though, when you realize that it's ok to want people to like you.

i dressed oddly and acted weird in high school, but made up pseudo-philosophical explanations about why i did it and how it wasn't for attention, but the truth is that i was trying to be someone who i (and by extension, others) found interesting. where once i would shamefully leave out an open sketchbook for someone to notice, hoping to trick people into being impressed with me, i can now show someone a comic i've made, point to it and say, "i'm proud of this."

the other thing is this horror at having grown up without accomplishing anything, and i think it's related to a sense of entitlement we grew up with.

when we were teenagers, we were the ones who thought we were literally too cool for school, thought we would run off when we turned 18, thought we were made for greatness. we are of a class of society with greater than average minds and ambitions, but just beneath the class of people we saw ourselves as, because we lack the strength of character to admit what we want and a willingness to work hard for it.

i used to tell myself it was an epidemic in our age group, a side effect of the escalation of this american dream mentality, but it's not. there are people our age who are accomplishing things, just like there are people from other generations who slipped through the cracks and felt as disillusioned as we do.

(this is what appealed to me about the movie revolutionary road, and why i was subsequently disappointed in the empty, convenient conclusion).

if i had the balls to pursue writing, that's what i would write about.

we are elitists who have never done anything to qualify as elite. we are artists who do not make art. we are not brave enough or sure enough to capitalize on our potential, so we let our potential serve in the void of accomplishment, satisfied to hold onto a secret that makes us feel superior. but not really satisfied, not under the surface, not when we stop swimming and let ourselves reflect.

anyway, you did a brave thing and i thought it warranted both recognition and discussion. sorry if i contribute to your self-concsiousness sometimes. i know we riff on each other a lot, but i don't mean it; you're someone i respect.
